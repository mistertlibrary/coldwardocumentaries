Research Toolkit

Cold War Innovations
Documentary Archive

The Cold War did not feel like history to the people living inside it. It felt like weather — permanent, structural, the condition under which everything else happened. This toolkit is an invitation to look at that weather from the inside out: to find the primary sources, the documents and photographs and films that people made and used and feared during those decades, and to let what you find shape what you say. The argument comes after the evidence. It always does, when the work is done honestly.

Researcher’s Toolkit
Toolkit · 01

Finding the Archive

An archive is not a library. A library gives you the book someone wrote after thinking carefully about what happened. An archive gives you what people made while things were happening — the photograph taken the morning after, the memorandum that never reached its recipient, the film produced to persuade an audience that no longer exists. The distance between those two kinds of documents is where historical thinking lives. This section points you toward the primary sources: the original photographs, declassified records, government films, and firsthand accounts from which everything else is eventually derived. Go slowly. The collections reward precision and punish impatience. What you are looking for may be filed under a name you haven’t thought of yet.

Major Open-Access Collections

These collections are free, legal, and built for public use. No login required for most; high-resolution downloads available. Verify each item’s license before incorporating it into your film. Some assets are cleared for any use. Others have conditions. Read the fine print.

Primary · Photos & Film
Library of Congress — Digital Collections
Your largest domestic intelligence asset: a catalog of over 20 million records, with millions of items digitized and freely accessible online. The Prints & Photographs division and the Motion Picture collection are essential. Search by subject heading, not just keyword. The archive rewards precision.
loc.gov/collections
Primary · Government Records
National Archives (NARA)
The official record of U.S. government activity. Declassified documents, agency memoranda, official photographs. If the federal government created it and it can now be seen, this is where to look. Search the Access to Archival Databases (AAD) tool for cross-agency records.
catalog.archives.gov
Primary · Film & Audio
Prelinger Archives (Internet Archive)
Surveillance footage from inside the culture itself: educational films, civil defense films, industrial newsreels from the 1940s–60s. Most items are public domain and downloadable. Highly watchable. Highly usable.
archive.org/details/prelinger
Primary · Art & Objects
Smithsonian Open Access
5.1 million+ digital items from across all Smithsonian museums, free for any use. Especially rich for science, technology, and American art history. No clearance required. Download, attribute, and proceed.
si.edu/openaccess
Mixed · All Media Types
Wikimedia Commons
A massive, reasonably well-organized collection of public domain and openly licensed media. Read the license on each file: not all assets are cleared for the same operations. Excellent for finding images that are already identified, attributed, and ready to use.
commons.wikimedia.org
Primary · Photos & Documents
NYPL Digital Collections
The New York Public Library’s free digital collections. Exceptional holdings in American social history, photography, and print culture. A reliable field asset for the postwar period.
digitalcollections.nypl.org
Secondary/Primary · Documents
Cold War Int’l History Project (Wilson Center)
Declassified documents from U.S. and Soviet archives, plus expert analysis. The other side’s files, now accessible. Essential for any topic where understanding both sides of the standoff is the whole point.
wilsoncenter.org
Primary · Documents & Treaties
Avalon Project (Yale Law School)
The canonical open-access collection of diplomatic and legal primary documents, maintained by Yale’s Lillian Goldman Law Library. Truman Doctrine, Marshall Plan text, NATO treaty, Cuban Missile Crisis documents, Geneva Accords, and the Oppenheimer AEC hearing: the founding texts of the Cold War’s formal structure. No login required.
avalon.law.yale.edu/subject_menus/coldwar.asp

AP / Getty Images (Editorial)
The largest commercial press photography archive in the world: invaluable, but not free. Getty’s embed tool allows certain images to appear on non-commercial websites and blogs without a license — but embedding into a documentary film requires a license. For film use, identify the image and pursue licensing, or find a public domain equivalent. Do not download and re-upload.
gettyimages.com/editorial-images

How to Search Archival Collections

The most common mistake: typing one broad keyword, getting thousands of results, and giving up. Archivists organize collections using controlled vocabulary — specific subject headings that may not match everyday language. Learn to search the way the catalog was built. Your results will improve immediately.

Search Strategies That Actually Work

  • Set a date range immediately. Filter before you browse. Broad results are the enemy of good research.
  • Use subject headings, not just keywords. Subject headings are the controlled vocabulary archivists use to describe things; they often differ from everyday language. When you find a useful record, click its subject tags to find related items the keyword search would never surface.
  • Filter by format before browsing: photographs, motion pictures, sound recordings, government documents. Know what you need before you look.
  • Try Boolean operators. AND narrows (topic AND related term). NOT excludes. OR widens. Combine them.
  • When keywords fail, try synonyms. The catalog doesn’t know what you call things — only what the cataloger called them. Experiment.
  • Research is iterative. Your first search is not your final search. Follow footnotes, subject tags, and related-records links. Let the evidence lead.

The 80/20 Rule: Building with B-Roll

Your film has a production requirement: 80% historical material (archival photographs, government films, newsreels, your expert interview), 20% student narrators on camera. This is not a bureaucratic ratio. It is the difference between a documentary and a recorded presentation. Gathering strong visual primary sources is the central research task — not an afterthought, not something you do after you write the script.

Build Your B-Roll Inventory First

Search the archives before you write your script. Find out what footage actually exists for your topic. Then write narration the evidence can support. A script written before the archive search produces voiceover that floats above unrelated images — a signal to every viewer that the filmmaker did not do the work.

Aim for 15–20 distinct visual assets before you begin editing. Log each one: source, title, date, license type, and where in your script it belongs.

B-Roll by Media Type: Where to Find It

  • Historical photographs: LOC Prints & Photographs, Smithsonian Open Access, NYPL Digital Collections, Wikimedia Commons
  • Newsreel & government footage: Prelinger Archives — your primary source for usable moving-image material
  • Newspaper front pages & headlines: Chronicling America covers through 1963. A well-composed close-up of a headline is legitimate B-roll.
  • Government documents: NARA and LOC. A document on screen communicates authenticity; declassified stamps communicate it even louder.
  • Artwork & design objects: Smithsonian Open Access, MoMA collection, LOC Prints & Photographs

The Interview: Preparing Your Expert Source

You must interview either a real expert (a subject-area teacher, scientist, or practitioner) or produce a well-researched mock interview with a historical figure. Both require serious preparation. A strong interview delivers analysis, not summary. It provides a perspective your script alone cannot: a depth of detail, an interpretive angle, a voice that is not yours.

Real Expert Interview
Identify a teacher whose discipline intersects your topic: Physics for the Space Race, Economics for franchising, English for The Crucible. Share your research questions, not just your topic — you want them to speak from expertise, not recite facts back at you. Prepare 5–8 questions in advance. The best questions are open-ended and discipline-specific. They cannot be answered with a yes or a no.
Mock Historical Interview
A mock interview is not improvised dialogue. Research your subject deeply enough to represent their documented views accurately; then dramatize positions they actually held, in language consistent with primary sources. Cite your sources for every claim the “figure” makes. This format works best for figures with well-documented public records: Miller’s HUAC testimony, Salk’s press statements, Eisenhower’s farewell address.
What Makes a Strong Question
Strong questions demand analysis, not summary. They connect to the interviewee’s specific expertise; they cannot be deflected with a yes or a no; they reveal something. Weak: “Why was Sputnik important?” Strong: “What does orbital mechanics tell us about the engineering gap NASA had to close after 1957?” The difference is preparation.
Production Note

Record a short pre-interview to check audio levels. The best material often arrives in the middle of a longer answer. Edit selectively; do not use the interview uncut. Forty-five seconds of precise expert analysis outperforms three minutes of general commentary every time.

Copyright, Fair Use & the Documentary

Copyright law is not, at its root, a bureaucratic inconvenience. It is an attempt to answer a genuinely hard question: when someone makes something, what do they owe the public, and what does the public owe them? The answers the law has settled on are imperfect and contested, and they change over time — the public domain grows by one year every January 1st, as works whose term has expired return to common ownership. Understanding the basics matters for two reasons. The practical one: you cannot use what you cannot legally use. The deeper one: a researcher who understands why these rules exist makes better decisions at the margins, where the rules run out and judgment begins.

What Copyright Covers (and What It Doesn’t)

Copyright protects original creative works (photographs, films, recordings, written texts, artworks) from the moment of their creation. You need permission or a license to use copyrighted material, unless an exception applies.

Copyright does not protect facts, ideas, historical events, or government documents produced by federal employees as part of their official duties. This is why NARA and LOC materials are often in the public domain: federal government output largely belongs to the public by law. This is your best asset as a documentary filmmaker.

Key Concept — Public Domain

Works in the public domain are free for any use — no license, no attribution required (though attributing them is still good scholarly practice). In the U.S., works published through 1930 are generally in the public domain (the cutoff advances by one year every January 1st). Works published from 1931 onward have varying copyright status depending on registration and renewal. Use the Cornell Copyright Chart to check.

Fair Use: What It Actually Means

Fair use (Section 107 of the U.S. Copyright Act) is a legal defense, not a blanket permission. It allows use of copyrighted material without a license in certain circumstances, evaluated case by case on four factors. All four must be weighed together. No single factor is decisive on its own.

Factor 1
Purpose & Character
Is your use transformative: does it add new meaning, commentary, or criticism? Educational, nonprofit uses weigh in your favor. Reproducing something solely to avoid a licensing fee does not.
Factor 2
Nature of the Work
Is the original work factual (a news photograph, a government report) or highly creative (a painting, a song, a novel)? Using factual works tilts toward fair use. Highly creative works receive stronger protection.
Factor 3
Amount Used
Did you use a small portion, or the entire work? Reproducing a full song, article, or film clip weighs against fair use. A brief excerpt used to make a specific analytical point weighs in your favor.
Factor 4
Market Effect
Does your use harm the market for the original? This is the most heavily weighted factor. A student film submitted privately has minimal market impact. That same film posted publicly on YouTube is a different calculation entirely.
Common Misconception

“Educational use” is not a magic phrase. It is one element of Factor 1: not a blanket clearance, not a get-out-of-jail-free card. The four factors still apply. A publicly posted film using a copyrighted song is a real legal exposure, regardless of the school context.

Reading Creative Commons Licenses

Many items in open archives carry Creative Commons (CC) licenses: not fully copyrighted, not fully public domain. Read the specific license on each item. Do not assume. The label on the tin matters.

CC0 / Public Domain Mark
No rights reserved. Use freely for any purpose. Attribution is good practice but not required.
CC BY
Use freely, including commercially. You must credit the creator with name, source, and license.
CC BY-SA
Use freely, but your derivative work must carry the same license. Good for student projects.
CC BY-NC
Use for non-commercial purposes only. Student films for school use likely qualify, but check carefully.
CC BY-ND
You can use and redistribute, but cannot modify or create derivatives. A clip in your timeline is a derivative — use with caution.
All Rights Reserved (©)
No permission granted. Requires a license or a defensible fair use argument. Document your reasoning if you proceed.
Practical Rule

When in doubt, choose the safer source. There is almost always a public domain or openly licensed alternative to any copyrighted image. Spend ten minutes searching the LOC or Wikimedia Commons before deciding you “need” a copyrighted image.

Always log every source you use (title, creator, date, URL, license type), even if your film doesn’t include formal citations. That record protects you and makes your work documentable.

Topic Index

Each card in this section begins with a framing question — not an answer, not a thesis, but an opening. That is deliberate. The best historical arguments begin not with conclusions looking for support but with genuine puzzles: why did this happen this way, what were people thinking, what did they not yet know that we now do? The discipline tags tell you which analytical threads run deepest through each topic, but they are not boundaries. A card tagged for science will almost always have an economic dimension; a card tagged for art will almost always raise questions about who was excluded from the story being told. The sources listed are starting points. Follow the footnotes. Let the evidence take you somewhere the framing did not anticipate. That is usually where the most interesting work begins.

Science & Technology
01
The Nuclear Age
SCIMATHLIT

What is remarkable about Mutual Assured Destruction is not that it was thought of, but that it was thought of and then adopted anyway. The logic is not difficult to follow: if both sides can survive a first strike and retaliate devastatingly, neither side will strike first, and the deterrence holds. What the logic requires you to believe is that the people managing these weapons, on both sides, under the stress of real crises, will always remain rational — that no miscalculation, no misread signal, no leader under pressure at three in the morning will make a different calculation. The name itself was coined in 1962 by Donald Brennan, a Hudson Institute strategist who opposed the doctrine and chose the acronym deliberately to argue that the policy was irrational. His opponents adopted the name anyway. The insult became the official posture of two nuclear superpowers for decades. That is the kind of detail that repays sustained attention.

Starter Sources
  • NSA Archive: The Nuclear Vault — Declassified planning documents, targeting lists, and strategic assessments from U.S. and Soviet archives. The deepest public primary source collection on weapons development and arms race strategy.
  • Atomic Heritage Foundation: Nuclear Testing History — Primary documents, photographs, and oral histories from the Manhattan Project through the hydrogen bomb program. Now part of the National Museum of Nuclear Science and History.
  • LOC: Nuclear Arms Race, 1945–1965 — Press photographs, civil defense publications, and government materials documenting the public face of the arms race.
Archival Leads

The Wilson Center Cold War International History Project has Soviet-side documents on weapons development — essential for a balanced account. The RAND Corporation’s history page (rand.org/about/history.html) links to declassified Cold War nuclear strategy reports, including original game theory and deterrence documents by Herman Kahn and others. (Note: Kahn was a critic of MAD, not its architect; his work argued for flexible, survivable nuclear strategies rather than mutual destruction.) Search Prelinger Archives for “civil defense” and “atomic bomb” for public domain government films.

02
The Space Race
SCIMATHARTLIT

There is something worth sitting with in the fact that the most consequential technological shock of the twentieth century announced itself as a sound. Not a photograph, not a document, not a treaty — a beep, transmitted from orbit on October 4, 1957, at a frequency any amateur radio operator could receive. Sputnik was the size of a beach ball and it contained, essentially, a radio transmitter and a thermometer. What it changed was not the physical balance of the Cold War, which remained as it had been, but the psychological and political one. The question it planted in American minds — if they can do that, what else can they do? — reorganized federal priorities for a decade, from the education budget to the defense appropriations to the structure of the university system. A small metal sphere beeping in the dark produced, among many other things, the National Defense Education Act, the research university as we now understand it, and eventually the Apollo program. One signal. Enormous downstream consequences. That kind of cascade is worth studying carefully.

Starter Sources
  • NASA History Division — The definitive open-access source: primary documents, mission reports, oral histories, and image galleries covering Sputnik, Explorer 1, Project Mercury, Saturn V, and Apollo. Most materials are public domain.
  • Smithsonian Air and Space Museum: Collections — Object photography and artifact records for Sputnik replicas, Mercury capsules, Saturn V hardware, and Apollo equipment. Open Access; high-resolution downloads available.
  • NSA Archive: Space Policy Collection — Declassified NSC discussions, intelligence assessments, and Soviet-side documents covering the full arc of the Space Race from Sputnik through the moon landing.
Archival Leads

Search Prelinger Archives for “Project Mercury,” “Explorer satellite,” and “NASA” for public domain government films. The NCES historical data (nces.ed.gov) documents the post-Sputnik education expansion under the National Defense Education Act (1958). LOC Chronicling America has contemporaneous newspaper coverage of every major launch.

03
Concrete Continent
SCIMATHART

Infrastructure has a way of making its purposes invisible once it becomes ordinary. Forty-one thousand miles of interstate highway now simply constitute the country — the frame inside which American economic and social life is arranged — and it takes an effort of historical imagination to remember that it was built, by specific people, for specific reasons, within a specific political moment. Eisenhower championed the system primarily on grounds of safety and economic development; civil defense arguments circulated in the legislative debate, and the act’s official title includes the word “Defense,” but scholarship has shown that the president himself said relatively little about nuclear evacuation when advocating for the plan. What the system actually produced — the suburb, the franchise, the drive-in, the commute, the particular shape of American loneliness — was downstream from intentions that were themselves contested and mixed. That gap between what policymakers intended and what the built environment eventually made possible is one of the richest questions in postwar American history.

Starter Sources
  • Federal Highway Administration: Interstate History — Official FHWA history of the system, including the 1956 Act, original planning maps, and Eisenhower’s role. Primary documents and construction photographs.
  • Eisenhower Presidential Library: Online Documents — Digitized records from Eisenhower’s presidency, including highway planning documents and the National Security justification for the interstate system.
  • LOC: Interstate Highway, 1950–1965 — Aerial photographs, engineering drawings, and press coverage of construction. Search also “suburbanization” for the domestic development context.
Archival Leads

Search NARA for Bureau of Public Roads records (Record Group 30). The LOC Prints & Photographs division has exceptional aerial photography of highway construction across the country. Prelinger Archives has promotional films from the Federal Highway Administration. Eisenhower’s 1919 military convoy report (a November 1919 memorandum to the Chief of the Motor Transport Corps, available via the Eisenhower Library) and his WWII experience with the Autobahn are key primary sources for understanding why he championed the system.

04
The Silicon Revolution
SCIMATHART

When Bell Labs demonstrated the transistor in June 1948, the New York Times covered the announcement in a few paragraphs on page 46 of the radio section. This is not a story about journalistic failure; it is a story about how difficult it is to recognize a general-purpose technology at the moment of its emergence. The transistor — a semiconductor amplifier built initially from two gold contacts pressed into a slab of germanium — did not look, in 1947, like the thing that would eventually run the missiles, the moon shots, the early computers, the transistor radio, and the propaganda apparatus of the Cold War. It looked like a laboratory curiosity. The distance between that first hand-built device and the integrated circuit is one of the clearest illustrations in modern history of how technical change works: not by announcement, but by accumulation.

Starter Sources
  • Computer History Museum Collections — Oral histories, artifacts, and technical documents on ENIAC, the transistor, integrated circuits, and early IBM mainframes. Searchable online with strong Cold War-era holdings.
  • Internet Archive: AT&T / Bell Labs Films — Bell Labs produced exceptional public-domain educational films explaining the physics of solid-state electronics and the transistor’s development.
  • Columbia University: IBM 701 Computing History — Detailed institutional history of the IBM 701 Defense Calculator (1952), with technical documentation and context on its military applications.
Archival Leads

The Nobel Prize website documents the 1956 Physics Prize (Bardeen, Brattain, Shockley for the transistor) with primary materials. Patent filings for the transistor and integrated circuits are available via Google Patents and are public domain. The Smithsonian National Museum of American History holds Bell Labs and IBM artifacts; search collections at americanhistory.si.edu or collections.si.edu. Note that while metadata for many objects carries a CC0 designation, individual photographs may have separate restrictions — check the usage conditions on each object record before downloading. Search LOC for “semiconductor industry” and “electronic computers — history.”

05
The Electric Living Room
SCIARTLIT

Consider what it meant, in 1955, for roughly two-thirds of American households to own a television set. Not just that they had a new appliance — though the appliance itself had a history worth tracing, from prewar research through wartime electronics manufacturing to the postwar consumer market — but that for the first time in history, a centrally produced moving image could appear simultaneously in tens of millions of private homes. The living room became something new: a private space that was also, at the moment the set was on, a shared public one. What went into the box — the news programs, the variety shows, the advertisements, the civil defense announcements — shaped how the Cold War looked and felt to people who had no other way of knowing what was happening. That is not a minor development in the history of the period. It is close to the center of it.

Starter Sources
  • Smithsonian Open Access: Domestic Technology — Search “television,” “microwave oven,” “color television,” and “RCA” for artifact photography and object records spanning the late 1940s through the 1960s.
  • LOC: Television & Advertising, 1948–1965 — Press photographs, TV Guide covers, and advertising imagery documenting the transformation of the American living room and television’s role in Cold War culture.
  • Prelinger Archives: Television Industry Films — Search “RCA,” “color television,” and “kitchen of the future” for promotional and educational films from broadcasters and appliance manufacturers.
Archival Leads

The “Kitchen Debate” (Nixon and Khrushchev, July 1959) directly connects domestic appliance technology to Cold War ideology — search NARA and the Nixon Presidential Library for footage and transcripts. The LOC’s NBC Television Collection (18,000 programs, 1948–1977; access restricted to on-site use at the LOC) and the American Archive of Public Broadcasting (americanarchive.org), a GBH–LOC collaboration with an online reading room, cover the early television industry. Search Chronicling America for contemporaneous TV criticism and advertising copy.

06
The Polio Vaccine
SCIMATHARTLIT

The 1954 polio field trial enrolled nearly two million children across the United States — at that point the largest controlled medical experiment in history — and the announcement of its results on April 12, 1955 produced something genuinely unusual: a moment of shared national feeling organized around a scientific finding. When Thomas Francis Jr. concluded his press conference at the University of Michigan and the news went out by wire and radio and television, people wept in the streets. Church bells rang. This is worth pausing on. It was not a military victory or a political event; it was a public health announcement. What had happened was not just the defeat of a disease but the confirmation of a belief — that organized, rational, collective effort could solve problems that had previously seemed beyond human control. That belief, in the mid-1950s, was doing a great deal of cultural work. The trial’s statistical design — two different methodologies running simultaneously in different states — is, separately, a masterclass in how the choices made in designing an experiment shape the conclusions it can support.

Starter Sources
  • NLM Digital Collections: Polio & Public Health History — Search “polio,” “Salk,” or “March of Dimes” in the National Library of Medicine’s digitized collections. Includes photographs, films, and publications from the 1954 field trial and 1955 announcement.
  • LOC: Polio, 1950–1960 — Photographs of the March of Dimes campaign, vaccination lines, and Salk press coverage. Rich for the Art/Design thread.
  • Internet Archive: Polio & Public Health Films — Search “polio,” “vaccination,” and “public health” for government-produced films in the public domain. Note: March of Dimes is a private organization; their own films are copyrighted and not confirmed freely available. Government public health films on related topics are the reliable route to usable B-roll.
Archival Leads

The 1954 field trial was the largest controlled medical experiment in history at that point — and its statistical design is essential for the Math thread. The trial actually used two different designs simultaneously: an “observed control” approach in 33 states (vaccinated second-graders versus unvaccinated peers), and a double-blind, placebo-controlled randomized trial in 11 states. The contrast between these designs and the debate over which was more rigorous became a landmark case in the history of clinical statistics. The March of Dimes Archives (White Plains, NY) holds correspondence, photography, film, and materials from the 1954 field trial; researchers may contact the archivist directly (marchofdimes.org). LOC has strong press photography of both the field trial and the 1955 announcement. Search Smithsonian for “public health — United States” objects.

07
The Environmental Crisis & Movement
SCILITMATH

Rachel Carson did not discover the problem she wrote about in Silent Spring. Industrial contamination of water, air, and food supplies had been accumulating for generations, and scientists had been documenting it. What Carson did was translate that scientific record into prose that general readers could follow and feel — a work of synthesis and persuasion addressed to people who were not, for the most part, following the technical literature. The chemical industry understood immediately what was at stake. Their effort to discredit her — personally, professionally, and scientifically — is itself a primary source, a case study in how economic interests respond to research that threatens them. It did not succeed. Within a decade, the legislative landscape had changed: the Clean Air Act, the Water Quality Act, the establishment of the EPA, the eventual ban on DDT. What “Silent Spring” did was not discover a problem but create the political conditions for addressing one. That is a distinct achievement, and worth understanding carefully.

Starter Sources
  • LOC: Rachel Carson & the Environmental Movement, 1955–1970 — Photographs, press coverage, and government publications documenting both Carson’s work and the legislative response. Search also “DDT,” “air pollution,” and “water pollution.”
  • Smithsonian Open Access: Natural History & Conservation — Search “environmental movement,” “conservation,” and “Rachel Carson” for objects, exhibition materials, and archival photographs documenting the emergence of environmentalism as a popular movement.
  • Internet Archive: Environmental Documentary Films — Government and advocacy films filtered to key years (1950, 1961, 1963, 1966–1969) bracketing Silent Spring and the first wave of environmental legislation. Strong B-roll potential.
Archival Leads

Rachel Carson’s papers are held at the Beinecke Rare Book & Manuscript Library (Yale); a finding aid is available online. Silent Spring itself (1962) is the central primary text — available via library catalog. The Senate committee hearings on pesticides that followed publication are available via Congress.gov as congressional hearing records — search “pesticides” and “Ribicoff” to find the 1963 hearings at which Carson testified. For the Math thread, the statistical data on DDT accumulation and species decline that Carson drew on came from USDA research and published scientific literature from the 1950s — search JSTOR for peer-reviewed studies on organochlorine pesticides and wildlife; the EPA’s own history page (epa.gov) documents the regulatory response from 1970 onward. The Clean Air Act (1963) and Water Quality Act (1965) are the direct legislative outcomes — both available via Congress.gov.

08
McDonald’s & the Franchise Empire
MATHARTLIT

The McDonald brothers had a successful restaurant. What Ray Kroc had was a theory about how to make that restaurant infinitely reproducible — a system of standardized inputs, standardized processes, and standardized outputs that could be replicated anywhere, by anyone, without depending on the skill or judgment of the person doing the work. This was not a new idea in manufacturing. It was a new idea in food service, and it turned out to be a genuinely transformative one. The product — the hamburger, the french fry — was almost incidental to the innovation. What Kroc invented was a method of converting locally owned, skill-dependent businesses into interchangeable units of a centrally controlled system. The postwar franchise economy, which eventually extended well beyond food into hotels, rental cars, tax preparation, and dozens of other service industries, was built on that template. Understanding why that template was so legible to the postwar American economy requires understanding what the postwar American economy was doing and why.

Starter Sources
  • LOC: McDonald’s & Fast Food, 1950–1965 — Advertising imagery, trade press coverage, and franchise documentation from the Kroc era.
  • Searching for McDonald’s footage: McDonald’s-specific promotional films from the Kroc era are not reliably in the public domain. For usable B-roll context, search the Prelinger Archives for “food service,” “drive-in restaurant,” and “supermarket” — these government and industrial films document postwar food culture and franchise-style operations without the copyright complications of branded content.
  • FRASER (St. Louis Fed): Postwar Economic Data — Economic indicators and Federal Reserve reports documenting the growth of the service economy alongside manufacturing in the postwar period.
Archival Leads

Ray Kroc’s memoir Grinding It Out (1977) is a primary source available via library catalog. Vance Packard’s The Hidden Persuaders (1957) offers contemporaneous cultural criticism of the mass marketing machine. For the Math thread, the standardized food production model’s cost and throughput data were covered in trade and business press of the era — search FRASER for relevant industry economic data from the 1950s and 1960s.

09
Disney & the Theme Park
MATHARTLIT

The opening day of Disneyland, on July 17, 1955, was a near-disaster. The park overheated in the Anaheim summer. Rides broke down. A plumbers’ strike had left the drinking fountains dry. Women’s heels sank into fresh asphalt. The television cameras, which were there because Disney had made the opening into a live ABC broadcast, captured all of it. And within a year, Disneyland was the most visited attraction in the United States. This gap between the chaotic reality and the cultural meaning that accrued to the place almost immediately is itself worth examining. What Disney had built was not merely an amusement park but something genuinely new in the world: a physical environment designed, from the ground up, to produce a specific emotional experience in the people moving through it. The street widths were calculated to feel welcoming without feeling grandiose. The sightlines were managed so that nothing outside the park was visible from within it. Every element of the guest experience was scripted. This was, among other things, a theory of narrative made architectural — and it arrived in the same moment as the television set, the interstate highway, and the franchise restaurant.

Starter Sources
  • LOC: Disneyland & Disney, 1950–1965 — Press photographs, newspaper coverage, and advertising materials from the 1955 opening and the first decade of operation.
  • LOC: Disneyland Opening & Reception, 1955 — Press photographs, magazine coverage, and government publications documenting the opening and public reception. Note: Disney’s ABC television series (1954–1961) remains under copyright and is not available as public domain footage. For B-roll context on postwar leisure and theme park culture, search Prelinger Archives for “amusement park” and “leisure” as public domain alternatives.
  • Smithsonian Open Access: Entertainment & Design — Search “Walt Disney,” “theme park,” and “Imagineering” for design objects, concept art, and exhibition materials.
Archival Leads

Chronicling America has contemporaneous newspaper coverage of Disneyland’s opening and reception. Richard Schickel’s The Disney Version (1968) is the essential critical secondary text. For the Math thread, Disneyland’s attendance and financial records from the first decade are cited in multiple business history sources — FRASER has postwar entertainment industry economic data.

Levittown: Blueprint of Suburbia
MATHARTLIT

William Levitt applied manufacturing logic to home construction — breaking the building process into 27 discrete steps, assigning specialized crews to each step, and moving them from lot to lot the way an assembly line moves product from station to station. By 1951 he had built 17,000 houses on Long Island at a peak rate of 30 per day. This was a genuine engineering achievement, and it made homeownership accessible to a generation of working-class families who could not previously have afforded it. It is essential to hold both things at once: the achievement and what it excluded. Every Levittown deed covenant contained a clause prohibiting occupancy by anyone who was not “a member of the Caucasian race.” The government mortgage programs that made Levittown possible operated on similar terms across the country. The postwar suburban expansion — one of the largest and fastest transfers of wealth in American history — was structured, from the beginning, as a transfer available primarily to white families. That is not incidental to the story. It is the story.

Starter Sources
  • LOC: Levittown & Suburbia, 1945–1965 — Aerial photographs, press coverage, and advertising materials documenting construction and cultural reception.
  • DPLA: Levittown, Pennsylvania Collections — Aggregates digitized materials from Pennsylvania repositories; search for photographs, maps, and documents from the Bucks County community.
  • Internet Archive: Postwar Housing Films — Search “Federal Housing Administration” and “GI Bill housing” for mid-century promotional and documentary films in the public domain.
Archival Leads

Kenneth Jackson’s Crabgrass Frontier (1985) is the essential secondary source. Search LOC for “redlining” and “restrictive covenants” — this is essential historical context, not optional. FRASER has demographic and housing start data from the postwar boom for the Math thread. The NAACP archives (LOC Manuscript Division) document resistance to racial exclusion in postwar suburbs; the collection is extensive but requires an in-person LOC reader card and is not available online — direct students to Katznelson and Jackson as the accessible route to this history.

GI Joe: The GI Bill
MATHLITART

The Servicemen’s Readjustment Act of 1944 is often described as the legislation that built the postwar American middle class, and at the scale of aggregate statistics, that description is defensible. Approximately 2.3 million veterans attended college on GI Bill benefits. Millions more financed homes, started businesses, received vocational training. The transformation of American higher education and American homeownership in the decade after the war is, in considerable part, a GI Bill story. What that aggregate description obscures is that the bill’s administration — through local Veterans Administration offices, local banks, and local university admissions offices — systematically denied Black veterans access to the same benefits. The same legislation, operating through the same agencies, produced profoundly different outcomes depending on the race of the veteran applying. This was not an accident or an oversight. It was a structural feature of how the bill was written and administered. Ira Katznelson’s term for this, “affirmative action for white men,” is deliberately provocative, but it is historically precise.

Starter Sources
  • NARA: GI Bill Records — Original legislative records and Veterans Administration documentation, including statistical data on college enrollment, home loan usage, and small-business participation by demographic.
  • LOC: GI Bill, 1944–1960 — Press photographs, government posters, publications, and advertising documenting the bill’s implementation and public marketing. Rich for the Art/Design thread.
  • Internet Archive: Veterans Administration Films — Public domain films produced by the VA and military branches documenting postwar benefits, rehabilitation, and veterans’ services. Search also “GI bill” and “returning veterans” within the Prelinger collection.
Archival Leads

The original text of the Servicemen’s Readjustment Act is available via Congress.gov. Ira Katznelson’s When Affirmative Action Was White (2005) is essential for understanding the racial disparities in implementation — the NAACP archives (LOC Manuscript Division) document Black veterans’ exclusion from many benefits, and this counternarrative is essential context — the collection requires an in-person LOC reader card and is not available online; Katznelson’s book and the NARA milestone document are the accessible starting points for high school researchers. For the Math thread, FRASER (stlouisfed.org) has postwar housing start and college enrollment data broken down by year.

Little Houses: Suburbia & the Sun Belt
MATHARTLIT

Before mechanical air conditioning became widely available, the climate of the American South and Southwest was a genuine constraint on where people chose to live and what kinds of economic activity could be sustained there. Air conditioning removed that constraint, and in removing it, set in motion one of the most significant demographic redistributions in postwar American history. Population flowed toward Phoenix and Atlanta and Houston and Miami, bringing capital and industry with it, reshaping the political arithmetic of the country over the following decades. But air conditioning alone does not explain the Sun Belt boom. The GI Bill made homeownership accessible to a generation that had previously rented. The interstate highway system made dispersed suburban development viable. Federal defense spending — concentrated heavily in Southern and Western states — seeded local economies. Consumer culture supplied the aspiration: the good life, as postwar advertising defined it, required a yard and a car and a house in a neighborhood that looked like the future. All of these forces converged, and the country shifted.

Starter Sources
  • U.S. Census Bureau: Through the Decades — Population and housing data from the 1950 and 1960 censuses. Essential for visualizing the statistical scale of suburbanization and Sun Belt growth — strong data for the Math thread.
  • LOC: Postwar Suburbs & Sun Belt Growth — Aerial photographs, advertising, and press coverage documenting postwar residential expansion. Search also “suburban development,” “tract housing,” and individual state names (“Arizona,” “Florida,” “California”) for regional coverage.
  • Internet Archive: Postwar Suburban Life Films — Government and industry promotional films from the late 1940s through the 1960s documenting consumer life in new suburban communities.
Archival Leads

Lizabeth Cohen’s A Consumers’ Republic (2003) is the essential secondary text connecting consumer culture to suburban growth. For the Sun Belt specifically, search the LOC for “Arizona,” “California,” and “Florida” combined with “suburban development” and “population growth.” The FRASER database has regional economic data on the Sun Belt’s postwar expansion.

The Keynes to Success
MATHLIT

John Maynard Keynes argued, in the 1930s, that capitalist economies in depression could not necessarily recover through market mechanisms alone — that government spending could substitute for the private investment that had collapsed, stimulate demand, and restart the cycle of economic activity. By the 1950s, this idea had a complicated political life in the United States. Explicitly Keynesian policy was ideologically suspect, associated in the popular mind with socialism and with the New Deal programs that conservatives had spent two decades trying to undo. And yet the federal government was, in practice, spending at levels and in patterns that functioned as Keynesian stimulus: the Marshall Plan, the interstate highway program, the defense budget, the space program. The politics required that this spending be justified by other rationales — national security, civil defense, international competition — rather than as deliberate demand management. The result was a postwar economic expansion built on a theory that its architects largely declined to name in public. That gap between what was done and what it was called is one of the more instructive features of the Cold War political economy.

Starter Sources
  • FRASER (St. Louis Fed): Historical Economic Data — Digitized Federal Reserve reports, Treasury documents, and economic indicators from the postwar era. Search for Council of Economic Advisers reports and federal budget data from the Truman and Eisenhower administrations.
  • LOC: Marshall Plan & Economic Policy, 1947–1965 — Primary documents, photographs, and press coverage of the Marshall Plan and postwar economic policy. Essential for connecting Keynesian theory to Cold War foreign policy practice.
  • HathiTrust Digital Library: Economic History Texts — Out-of-copyright editions of Keynes’s The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money (1936) and contemporaneous policy debates are searchable here. Look also for contemporaneous critiques from Hayek and the Chicago School.
Archival Leads

This topic requires you to anchor abstract economic theory to concrete Cold War policy events to make it filmable. The best approach: choose one specific application — the Marshall Plan (1948), the National Defense Education Act (1958), or the Interstate Highway Act (1956) — and trace the Keynesian logic explicitly through that case. NARA holds original Marshall Plan administrative records (Record Group 469). The Eisenhower Library (eisenhowerlibrary.gov) has declassified Council of Economic Advisers reports. Contemporaneous debates between Keynesian and free-market economists are available via JSTOR (100 free reads/month).

Car Culture: The Chevy Corvette
ARTMATHLIT

When General Motors introduced the Corvette at the 1953 Motorama in New York, it was making a claim about what America was and what it wanted to become. The car itself — a fiberglass-bodied, European-inspired sports car unlike anything in the existing American market — was less important than what it represented: the idea that American industry could produce objects of desire, not merely objects of utility. The Motorama shows, held annually through most of the 1950s, were theater as much as automobile exhibition — elaborate productions staging a vision of the technological future as domestic and optimistic and available to anyone who could make the payments. This vision was doing cultural work. It was an argument about American modernity aimed at a domestic audience that had just won a world war and at an international one that was being asked to choose sides in a Cold War. Harley Earl, GM’s chief designer from 1927 to 1958, understood the car as a communication device. The tail fins were not engineering; they were rhetoric.

Starter Sources
  • Smithsonian Open Access: Automotive Design — Search “Chevrolet Corvette,” “automobile design 1950s,” and “Harley Earl” for objects, advertisements, and design drawings from the era.
  • Internet Archive: GM Motorama Films — General Motors’ Motorama shows (1953–1961) debuted the Corvette and “dream cars” to the public; promotional films document the space-age aesthetic.
  • LOC: Automobile Advertising & Design — Magazine advertisements, poster art, and press photography. Search also “Chevrolet,” “tail fins,” and “Harley Earl” for the specific visual language of postwar car culture.
Archival Leads

Harley Earl (GM’s chief designer, 1927–1958) is the central figure — search LOC and Smithsonian for primary materials on his design philosophy. For the “Googie” architecture angle, search LOC for “roadside architecture” and “commercial vernacular.” The Prelinger Archives have exceptional General Motors corporate films from this period.

Abstract Expressionism
ARTLIT

Abstract Expressionism is one of the few art movements whose institutional history includes a covert intelligence operation. Through the Congress for Cultural Freedom, a front organization funded by the CIA, American abstract painting was promoted internationally throughout the 1950s as evidence that the United States — unlike the Soviet Union, with its official Socialist Realism — allowed its artists genuine freedom of expression. The artists themselves were not, for the most part, informed of these arrangements. Pollock and Rothko and de Kooning were painting what they painted for their own reasons; the cultural diplomacy function was added by intermediaries who understood the strategic value of genuine creative freedom, even when that freedom produced work that was commercially marginal and formally difficult. The story raises questions that resist easy resolution: Can art be simultaneously authentic and instrumentalized? Does the covert promotion of genuine work compromise it? Frances Stonor Saunders’s research, drawing on declassified documents, provides the factual foundation; the interpretive questions remain open.

Starter Sources
  • MoMA Online Collection: AbEx, 1940–1965 — Browse and research specific works; collection data and artwork images accessible online. Essential for Pollock, Rothko, de Kooning, and Kline.
  • Smithsonian American Art Museum: Research — Open-access artwork images, exhibition records, and the Archives of American Art. The definitive institutional repository for artist papers and gallery records.
  • JSTOR Daily: “Was Modern Art Really a CIA Psy-Op?” — Well-sourced overview of the cultural diplomacy program connecting art history to Cold War politics. Free to access; draws on the same scholarly record as the Frances Stonor Saunders research.
Archival Leads

Key primary sources include Clement Greenberg’s “American-Type Painting” (1955) — collected in Art and Culture (Beacon Press, 1961; available via library catalog) — and Hans Namuth’s photographs and films of Pollock painting (1950), held at the Archives of American Art, Smithsonian Institution. Note: the Namuth collection requires an in-person appointment at the AAA’s Washington, D.C. Research Center; reproductions require rights clearance from the Center for Creative Photography (University of Arizona). The finding aid is available at aaa.si.edu. Frances Stonor Saunders’s The Cultural Cold War (2000) is the essential secondary text on the CIA connection. Meyer Schapiro’s contemporaneous essays — especially “The Liberating Quality of Avant-Garde Art” (ArtNews, Summer 1957) — document the critical reception; the essay is collected in Modern Art: 19th and 20th Centuries (Braziller, 1978) and available via library catalog.

Rock ’n’ Roll
ARTLITSCI

Rock ’n’ roll did not emerge from nothing in the mid-1950s. It had deep roots in Black American musical traditions — blues, rhythm and blues, gospel — that had been developing for decades, largely within segregated cultural spaces. What changed in the 1950s was not the music but the technology and the economics of its distribution. The transistor radio made music portable and personal, putting it in teenagers’ pockets and cars rather than in the family living room where adults controlled the dial. The recording industry began marketing rhythm and blues to white teenagers under the name “rock and roll.” The music crossed racial lines in a way that alarmed adults on both sides of the political spectrum: Southern segregationists saw it as a threat to the color line; Northern conservatives saw it as a symptom of moral decline. Neither analysis was entirely wrong about what the music was doing. It was disrupting the generational transmission of cultural authority, and it was doing so partly by making the segregation of American musical culture visible and audible as a choice rather than a natural condition.

Starter Sources
  • LOC: Rock ’n’ Roll & Popular Music, 1954–1965 — Photographs, sheet music, and press coverage of early rock ’n’ roll. Search also “payola,” “American Bandstand,” and “Sun Records.”
  • Internet Archive: Early Rock ’n’ Roll Television — Some kinescopes of lesser-known variety programs have entered the public domain; results are limited. Note: Ed Sullivan footage is held by SOFA Entertainment and is not in the public domain. Search Prelinger Archives for “teenager” and “youth culture” for usable B-roll context.
  • Smithsonian: American Music History — Search “rock and roll,” “rhythm and blues,” and “Elvis Presley” for instruments, recording equipment, and cultural artifacts documenting the genre’s material history.
Archival Leads

The LOC’s American Folklife Center holds recordings and oral histories documenting the roots of rock ’n’ roll in rhythm and blues and gospel traditions. The 1959–1960 Congressional payola hearings (Congress.gov) are a primary source on the industry’s political response to rock ’n’ roll’s crossover success. For the Science thread: the transistor radio’s role in distributing the music is a direct connection to Card 04.

The Crucible & HUAC
LITART

Arthur Miller wrote The Crucible in 1953 using the Salem witch trials as a frame for what was happening in contemporary America: the congressional investigations, the loyalty oaths, the naming of names, the destruction of careers on the basis of association and accusation. The allegory was not subtle, and it was widely understood as such. Three years later, HUAC summoned Miller himself to testify. He appeared and refused to name other people who had attended political meetings with him, and was subsequently cited for contempt of Congress. The playwright had become a figure in the situation he had dramatized, which is either a remarkable instance of history repeating itself as theater or a reminder that political allegory, when it is accurate, tends to remain accurate. What makes this episode worth sustained attention is not the irony but the mechanism: the way in which the committee’s power depended not on proving guilt but on requiring public demonstrations of loyalty, and the way in which refusing to perform that loyalty was itself treated as evidence of subversion.

Starter Sources
  • Congress.gov: Congressional Record — HUAC hearing transcripts are in the official congressional record, searchable by committee and date. Miller’s 1956 testimony and the Hollywood Ten hearings (1947) are here.
  • Internet Archive: HUAC Hearing Footage — Newsreel and broadcast coverage of the Hollywood hearings; some footage is in the public domain.
  • PBS American Masters: Miller’s HUAC Testimony — Excerpts from Arthur Miller’s 1956 testimony before the House Un-American Activities Committee, with context. A direct primary source, freely accessible.
Archival Leads

Miller’s 1953 essay “Journey to The Crucible” (New York Times) explains his own intentions in writing the play — a model of an author treating his work as historical argument. The Hollywood Ten’s statements are available via the Internet Archive. Dalton Trumbo’s correspondence (Wisconsin Center for Film and Theater Research) is an extraordinary primary source. Ellen Schrecker’s Many Are the Crimes (1998) is the standard secondary text.

The Atomic Screen
LITARTSCI

The science fiction films of the 1950s and early 1960s are sometimes treated as curios — low-budget entertainments that happen to reflect the anxieties of their moment. Susan Sontag’s 1965 essay “The Imagination of Disaster” offers a more rigorous account: these films were doing specific cultural work, managing a form of anxiety that could not be addressed directly and so found displaced expression in monsters, invasions, and catastrophes that were simultaneously about nuclear weapons and not about them. The creature from radiation testing. The alien invasion that required the suspension of individual judgment. The military-scientific establishment that turned out to be the real threat. These were not accidental metaphors; they were the forms available for thinking in public about things that could not easily be thought in public. The range is important: Invasion of the Body Snatchers (1956) has been read as an allegory for both Communist infiltration and McCarthyite conformity, and the fact that it sustains both readings simultaneously tells you something about the film and something about the period.

Starter Sources
  • Internet Archive: Public Domain Feature Films — Several key Cold War films are streamable and downloadable. Search “Invasion of the Body Snatchers,” “atomic,” and “science fiction 1950s” for usable footage.
  • LOC: Cold War Cinema, 1950–1965 — Film stills, lobby cards, and press materials. Search “Cinerama” and “widescreen” for the technology angle that connects to the Science thread.
  • JSTOR: Cold War Science Fiction Scholarship — Peer-reviewed analyses of Dr. Strangelove, Invasion of the Body Snatchers, and Cold War cinema. Essential secondary source material for this topic.
Archival Leads

Susan Sontag’s “The Imagination of Disaster” (Commentary, October 1965) is the essential critical essay on Cold War science fiction film — collected in Against Interpretation and Other Essays (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1966) and available via library catalog or library database. The HUAC Hollywood hearings (Congress.gov) provide political context for how studios responded to Cold War pressure. Search Chronicling America for contemporaneous film reviews and box office coverage.

The Beat Generation
LITART

The Beats are often misremembered as a unified movement with a coherent program. They were not. What they shared was less a politics than a sensibility — a refusal of the postwar social contract as it had been defined by the institutions that administered it: the corporation, the suburb, the university, the military. Kerouac’s restlessness, Ginsberg’s formal extremity, Burroughs’ systematic derangement of narrative — these were different responses to a shared diagnosis, which was that the apparent prosperity and stability of postwar American life was purchased at a cost that was not being acknowledged. Whether that diagnosis was accurate, and whether the Beats’ responses to it were adequate, is a genuine question. What is not in question is that the work they produced — and the public controversy it generated, from the Howl obscenity trial in 1957 to the mainstream critical contempt documented by Podhoretz — illuminates something real about the period’s contradictions: a culture that proclaimed freedom as its defining value and was made deeply uncomfortable when some of its members took that proclamation seriously.

Starter Sources
  • LOC: Beat Generation, 1950–1965 — Manuscript materials, photographs, and press coverage. Allen Ginsberg’s papers are held at Stanford University Libraries (finding aid online); the LOC holds recordings of Ginsberg reading his poetry, some digitized.
  • Internet Archive: Naropa Poetics Audio Archive — Over 5,000 hours of audio recordings from the Jack Kerouac School of Disembodied Poetics (founded 1974 by Allen Ginsberg and Anne Waldman). Includes Ginsberg reading Howl, lectures by Burroughs, and recordings of Gregory Corso, Diane di Prima, Philip Whalen, and others. The most significant open-access archive of Beat and post-Beat literary audio.
  • HathiTrust Digital Library: Beat Generation Scholarship — Out-of-copyright critical texts and contemporaneous literary journals. Search for early academic and journalistic responses to Kerouac and Ginsberg from the late 1950s.
Archival Leads

The Howl obscenity trial (San Francisco, 1957) generated extensive newspaper coverage — search Chronicling America for contemporaneous reporting. The Harry Ransom Center (UT Austin) holds manuscript materials for several Beat writers. Norman Podhoretz’s hostile contemporaneous critique “The Know-Nothing Bohemians” (Partisan Review, Spring 1958) is a key primary text for understanding the mainstream reaction — request via library interlibrary loan or look for it in the Podhoretz collection Doings and Undoings (1964).

Toolkit · 04

Interdisciplinary Source Finder

The Cold War was not, in any fundamental sense, separable into disciplines. The missile program required physicists and economists and propagandists and procurement officers, all working on the same problem from different angles, often without knowing what the others were doing. Your documentary faces an analogous challenge in miniature: the five threads — science and technology, mathematics and data, art and design, narrative and media, business and economy — are not five separate topics but five different lenses trained on the same historical moment. A single event, looked at through all five, becomes something richer and stranger than any one account could produce. This section points you toward the specialized databases for each thread. What you find in one column will almost always illuminate something you did not expect in another.

Science & Technology

The history of technology is, at bottom, the history of what becomes possible — and of who gets to decide how the possible becomes actual. A transistor is not just a switching device; it is also a decision about what kind of world to build, made by particular people in a particular institutional context, for particular reasons. The sources in this thread help you reconstruct that context: who funded the work, what problems they were trying to solve, how the results were understood at the time, and what they made possible next. The most interesting technical history is never just about the technology.

American Physical Society: APS News
Authoritative articles on major physics breakthroughs, including transistors and space science. The “This Month in Physics History” feature is particularly useful for Cold War-era topics. Free.
Science History Institute: Distillations
Long-form essays and podcasts on the history of science and technology. Strong on Cold War chemistry, materials science, and pharmaceutical history. Readable by non-specialists.
NASA History Division
Primary documents, technical reports, oral histories, and image galleries. The definitive open-access source for Space Race research. Many documents are declassified; most are downloadable.
Computer History Museum Collections
Oral histories, artifacts, and technical documents on computing history from ENIAC through the personal computer era. Searchable online; strong Cold War-era holdings.
HathiTrust Digital Library
Millions of digitized books and journals, including out-of-copyright technical publications from the postwar era. Search for contemporaneous scientific reports, policy documents, and textbooks.
Research Tip

When researching a technical innovation, find the original patent filing via Google Patents. Patent diagrams are public domain. They are also, often, the clearest explanation of how a technology actually worked — engineers write for engineers, not for posterity.

Math & Data

Numbers do not speak for themselves. They are made by people, for purposes, under conditions that shape what gets counted and what does not. The statistical record of the Cold War — the warhead inventories, the enrollment figures, the demographic surveys, the growth rates — is not a neutral substrate beneath the historical narrative; it is itself part of the narrative, produced by institutions with interests and methods that are themselves worth examining. The sources in this thread give you the numbers. Your job is to ask where they came from, who collected them, and what the collection process may have left out.

U.S. Census Bureau: Historical Statistics
Demographic data on suburbanization, education, income, and housing from the 1950 and 1960 censuses. Essential for Levittown, the GI Bill, and franchise economics. The numbers tell the story the narrative can only gesture at.
FRASER (St. Louis Fed): Historical Economic Data
Digitized Federal Reserve reports, budget documents, and economic indicators from the postwar era. Free, fully searchable, and deeply sourced.
RAND Corporation: History & Declassified Reports
RAND applied game theory and systems analysis to nuclear deterrence strategy during the Cold War. Many original reports are now publicly available, including work by Herman Kahn — a RAND analyst who argued nuclear war was survivable and opposed MAD as a credible deterrent. A foundational site for understanding how strategic analysis shaped nuclear policy.
Our World in Data: Nuclear Weapons
Citable visualizations of nuclear warhead stockpiles and arms race data. Useful for building data-driven documentary segments; each chart cites primary sources you can trace.
NCES: Digest of Education Statistics
Initiated in 1962 and published annually (with three combined editions: 1977–78, 1983–84, and 1985–86), the Digest includes historical tables on enrollment, federal education funding, and science and math course-taking from the postwar era through the present. Useful for documenting the post-Sputnik expansion funded by the National Defense Education Act (1958).
Research Tip

When you find a statistic: who collected it, why, and what does it leave out? Cold War-era numbers were frequently produced by parties with a stake in the results. The statistic is itself a primary source. Treat it accordingly.

Art & Design

Visual culture is ideology made physical. A Chevrolet advertisement from 1957 and a civil defense poster from the same year are both trying to produce a particular relationship between the viewer and the world — one of desire, one of fear, both in the service of a social order that needed people to feel specific things. The sources in this thread help you develop the habit of reading images the way a historian reads documents: attending not just to what is shown but to what is excluded, not just to the surface but to the choices that produced it. Every designed object from this period is an argument. Part of your job is to say what argument it is making, and for whom.

Smithsonian American Art Museum Research
Open-access artwork images, exhibition records, and the Archives of American Art. Essential for Abstract Expressionism and postwar design topics. Artist papers are often digitized and available here.
LOC Prints & Photographs Online Catalog
Over 17 million items: photographs, posters, architectural drawings, cartoons, prints. Filter by subject heading, date, and format. One of the deepest visual archives available to the public.
Google Arts & Culture: Cold War
High-resolution artwork and artifact images from museum partners worldwide. Useful for visual context. Check terms before downloading; licensing varies by institution.
Internet Archive: Ephemera Collection
Advertisements, pamphlets, and printed ephemera from the postwar era. Excellent for analyzing the visual culture of consumerism and Cold War anxiety in everyday graphic design: the medium is often the message.
Research Tip

When analyzing a visual source: who made it, for whom, in what context was it seen, and what does it ask its audience to believe or feel? A GM advertisement and a civil defense poster are both Cold War artifacts. They are doing very different ideological work. Your job is to say what that work is.

Narrative & Media

There is a version of the Cold War that you can reconstruct entirely from official documents, and it is a useful version — strategic, institutional, policy-driven. But there is another version that only emerges when you listen to what people were actually reading and watching and hearing while it happened. The newspaper on the morning after Sputnik. The science fiction film playing at the drive-in in 1956. The radio program that explained, to families eating dinner in the Midwest, what fallout was. These sources do not tell you what the government decided; they tell you what the culture feared and wanted and believed. That is a different kind of knowledge, and in some ways a more durable one. The sources in this thread help you reconstruct the texture of living through an event, rather than the abstracted record of what the event meant in retrospect.

Chronicling America (LOC)
Digitized U.S. newspapers from 1756 through 1963. Fully free and keyword-searchable. The contemporaneous record: what people read as events unfolded, not what historians said about them afterward.
Internet Archive: Audio & Radio
Thousands of digitized radio broadcasts from the postwar era. Primary sources for understanding how the Cold War sounded to American audiences. Voice, tone, and emphasis that text cannot replicate.
JSTOR: Cold War Literature & Media
Peer-reviewed scholarship on Cold War fiction, film, and media. Up to 100 free article reads per month without an institutional login; full access through your school’s library databases.
DPLA: Digital Public Library of America
Aggregates collections from libraries and archives across the U.S. Strong for finding digitized books, journals, and periodicals from the Cold War era. One search: many repositories.
Internet Archive: Feature Films
Public domain films, including Cold War-era science fiction and social problem films. Search by decade. Primary sources for the Narrative & Media thread.
Voices & Visions: U.S. Foreign Relations Reader
A collaboratively built, audio-visual primary source reader covering U.S. foreign relations from Theodore Roosevelt (1906) through the post-Cold War era (through 1999). Combines audio recordings, film clips, and images with interpretive context. Strong on civil defense culture, nuclear anxiety, and the foreign policy dimensions of the Cold War. Free.
Miller Center: Presidential Recordings Program (UVA)
Audio recordings and summaries from the Secret White House Tapes: FDR through Nixon. Kennedy’s Cuban Missile Crisis deliberations, LBJ’s Vietnam calls, Eisenhower’s internal debates — the unscripted record of presidential decision-making. Audio free; fully annotated transcripts available via the Presidential Recordings Digital Edition (UVA Press, subscription).
GBH Open Vault (Boston)
WGBH Boston’s archival broadcast journalism collection. The raw interview footage from War and Peace in the Nuclear Age (1989) — a thirteen-part PBS series on forty years of nuclear competition — is accessible online; full episodes require an in-person appointment at GBH in Boston. Note: the FRONTLINE collection at Open Vault is restricted to on-site and special-arrangement access; current FRONTLINE documentaries are freely available at pbs.org/frontline.
Suggested Texts by Topic

The Right Stuff, Tom Wolfe (Space Race) · On the Beach, Nevil Shute (Nuclear Anxiety) · The Man in the Gray Flannel Suit, Sloan Wilson (Suburbia) · Howl, Allen Ginsberg (Counterculture) · The Manchurian Candidate, Richard Condon (Surveillance) · The Crucible, Arthur Miller (McCarthyism)

Business & Economy

The postwar American economy is sometimes described as if prosperity simply happened — as if growth were natural, as if the middle class emerged the way weather does, from impersonal atmospheric forces. But the economic structures that shaped the postwar decades were built, deliberately, by identifiable people making identifiable decisions: to fund highways instead of railroads, to insure suburban mortgages and not urban ones, to channel defense contracts through particular industries in particular regions. The sources in this thread help you reconstruct the architecture of those decisions. Both numbers and corporate histories are required because neither alone is sufficient: the numbers without the narrative tell you what happened; the narrative without the numbers cannot tell you how much, how fast, or for whom.

FRASER (St. Louis Fed): Historical Economic Data
The single best open-access source for postwar economic data: Federal Reserve reports, Treasury documents, Council of Economic Advisers reports, economic indicators from the entire Cold War era. Free and fully searchable.
U.S. Census Bureau: Through the Decades
Population, housing, income, and consumer data from the 1940, 1950, and 1960 censuses. Essential for quantifying suburbanization, franchise growth, and the Sun Belt migration.
HathiTrust Digital Library: Business & Economic History
Useful for identifying and locating postwar business histories, trade publications, and economic texts. Full text is freely accessible for works in the public domain (generally published before 1931); most postwar titles are in limited view. Use HathiTrust to identify relevant titles, then request them through your school or public library.
RAND Corporation: History & Declassified Reports
RAND was the archetypal Cold War policy research institution — a nonprofit think tank, not a defense manufacturer, whose work on deterrence strategy, systems analysis, and cost-benefit modeling shaped how the U.S. government made decisions. Primary documents on defense economics available from the history page.
Eisenhower Presidential Library: Online Documents
Declassified records including Council of Economic Advisers reports, budget documents, and the internal debate over military spending versus civilian economic growth. Eisenhower’s farewell address — the one that coined “military-industrial complex” — is here. One of the era’s most important primary texts.
Research Tip

This thread works best when it is specific. “The economy grew” is not an argument. “McDonald’s franchise model produced X locations and $Y in revenue between 1955 and 1965, applying the same assembly-line logic as a defense factory” is an argument. Find the numbers first; build the analysis around them.
